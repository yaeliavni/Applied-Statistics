Statistical Sampling: Mathematical Foundations
Markov Chain Monte Carlo Theory

Both algorithms implement MCMC methods to sample from probability distributions π(x) where direct sampling is impossible. The goal is to construct a Markov chain with stationary distribution π(x).

Target: π(x) ∝ exp(-U(x)) where U(x) is the potential energy function

Detailed Balance Condition: For ergodic sampling, we need:

π(x)P(x→x') = π(x')P(x'→x) where P(x→x') is transition probability
Key Insight: Random Walk uses local proposals while HMC uses global proposals informed by the distribution's geometry through gradient information ∇U(x).
Metropolis-Hastings Framework

Both algorithms use the Metropolis-Hastings acceptance criterion to ensure convergence to the target distribution:

α(x→x') = min(1, π(x')q(x'→x) / π(x)q(x→x')) where q(x→x') is the proposal distribution
Random Walk
q(x→x') = N(x', σ²I) α = min(1, π(x')/π(x))

Symmetric proposals simplify acceptance ratio to likelihood ratio only.

HMC
q(x→x') from Hamiltonian dynamics α = min(1, exp(-H(x') + H(x)))

Volume-preserving dynamics maintain detailed balance with energy conservation.

Advanced Statistical Analysis: Convergence & Efficiency
Convergence Diagnostics

Effective Sample Size (ESS): Measures how many independent samples your correlated chain represents:

ESS = N / (1 + 2∑ρₖ) where ρₖ is lag-k autocorrelation

Autocorrelation Function:

ρₖ = Cov(Xₜ, Xₜ₊ₖ) / Var(Xₜ) Lower ρₖ → Higher ESS → Better mixing
HMC Advantage: Gradient information reduces autocorrelation by making distant proposals, typically achieving ESS/N ratios of 0.6-0.9 vs 0.1-0.3 for Random Walk.
Geometric Convergence Analysis

Spectral Gap: The second-largest eigenvalue λ₂ of the transition kernel determines convergence rate:

||P^n(x,·) - π||ₜᵥ ≤ C λ₂ⁿ Smaller λ₂ → Faster convergence

Optimal Scaling Theory:

Random Walk: Accept rate ≈ 0.44 (d-dimensional) HMC: Accept rate ≈ 0.65 (trajectory-dependent)

Computational Complexity:

  • Random Walk: O(1) per proposal
  • HMC: O(L) per proposal, where L is trajectory length
  • But HMC achieves ~L² effective sample size improvement
Efficiency Metric: ESS per gradient evaluation. HMC typically achieves 10-100x improvement in high-dimensional problems.
Treasure Hunt: Exploration Strategy Analysis
Search Strategy Mathematics

Exploration vs Exploitation Trade-off: Finding high-probability regions efficiently requires balancing exploration of unknown areas with exploitation of discovered high-value zones.

Utility(x) = P(treasure|x) × P(discovery|x) where P(treasure|x) ∝ π(x)

Random Walk Strategy:

E[discovery_time] ∝ d²/σ² where d is distance to target, σ is step size
Random Search Efficiency: Follows a diffusion process with exploration radius √(2Dt) where D is diffusion coefficient and t is time.
Gradient-Guided Search Theory

HMC Treasure Finding: Uses gradient ascent properties within MCMC framework to locate high-density regions systematically.

∇log π(x) = -∇U(x) Points toward probability mass

Information-Theoretic Advantage:

Information gain ∝ ||∇log π(x)||² Higher gradients → More directional information

Trajectory Optimization: Each HMC trajectory solves:

dq/dt = ∂H/∂p = p dp/dt = -∂H/∂q = -∇U(q)
Strategic Advantage: HMC trajectories follow contours of constant probability, systematically exploring high-density regions rather than wandering randomly.
HMC Algorithm Internals: Mathematical Decomposition
Hamiltonian Dynamics Foundation

Energy Function Construction: HMC augments the target distribution with auxiliary momentum variables:

H(q,p) = U(q) + K(p) where U(q) = -log π(q) + constant and K(p) = p^T M⁻¹ p / 2

Canonical Distribution:

π(q,p) ∝ exp(-H(q,p)) Marginal: ∫π(q,p)dp = π(q)

Leapfrog Integration Scheme:

p(t+ε/2) = p(t) - (ε/2)∇U(q(t)) q(t+ε) = q(t) + ε M⁻¹p(t+ε/2) p(t+ε) = p(t+ε/2) - (ε/2)∇U(q(t+ε))
Symplectic Property: Leapfrog preserves phase space volume, ensuring reversibility and maintaining detailed balance even with discretization errors.
Real-Time Decision Process

Gradient Computation: At each position q, compute force field:

F(q) = -∇U(q) = ∇log π(q) Numerical: F_i ≈ [U(q+hₑᵢ) - U(q-hₑᵢ)] / 2h

Energy Conservation Check:

ΔH = H(q*,p*) - H(q,p) Accept if: rand() < exp(-ΔH)

Trajectory Proposal Distribution:

q*(q,p) = Φₗ(q,p) where Φₗ is L-step leapfrog map p ~ N(0,M) independently resampled
Intelligence Mechanism: The gradient ∇U(q) encodes curvature information about π(q), allowing HMC to "see" probability landscape geometry and move efficiently toward high-density regions.
